About Diabetic Retinopathy Detection

What is Diabetic Retinopathy?

Diabetic retinopathy is a diabetes complication that affects the eyes. It's caused by damage to the blood vessels in the retina, the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye.

At first, diabetic retinopathy may cause no symptoms or only mild vision problems. Eventually, it can cause blindness. The condition can develop in anyone who has type 1 or type 2 diabetes. The longer you have diabetes and the less controlled your blood sugar is, the more likely you are to develop this eye complication.

Understanding DR Severity Levels

  • No DR

    No visible signs of diabetic retinopathy. Regular screening is still important.

  • Mild NPDR

    Mild nonproliferative diabetic retinopathy. Characterized by small areas of balloon-like swelling in the retina's tiny blood vessels.

  • Moderate NPDR

    As the disease progresses, some blood vessels that nourish the retina become blocked.

  • Severe NPDR

    Many more blood vessels are blocked, depriving several areas of the retina of their blood supply.

  • Proliferative DR

    The most advanced stage. The retina starts growing new blood vessels (neovascularization), which are fragile and can leak blood, potentially causing severe vision problems.

Technical Details

Our system uses deep learning to analyze fundus images for signs of diabetic retinopathy. Here's how it works:

  • Model Architecture: EfficientNet B0 - a convolutional neural network designed for image classification tasks
  • Ensemble Approach: Multiple models (5-fold cross-validation) vote on the final prediction, increasing accuracy and robustness
  • Image Processing: Each image is resized, normalized, and prepared for neural network analysis
  • Classification: The system classifies images into five severity levels based on features learned from thousands of training examples

Limitations

This tool is for educational and screening purposes only. It is not intended to replace professional medical diagnosis. Some limitations include:

  • Image quality affects results - poor quality images may lead to incorrect classification
  • The system only detects diabetic retinopathy, not other eye conditions
  • AI systems can make mistakes - always consult with healthcare professionals
